Main Use Case Theme

by Kevin Garwood

ALGAE was originally developed to support a specific environmental health study, and was later generalised both in response to the environment in which it was deployed and in response to growing interest from other environmental health researchers who may want to use it.

ALGAE was designed to use residential address histories of cohort study members to assess their exposures to various pollutants, using historically assessed exposure values within an exposure area. It was made to support notions of an early life analysis and a later life analysis to fit a time frame that is of interest in early life environmental health studies. The early life analysis begins at conception and will typically end at some point in the first year of life. The later life analysis begins at the date of birth and uses year long life stages that typically end at in the 16th year of life.

The protocol cleans the residential address histories so that study members occupy exactly one location for each day of their exposure time frame (e.g.: conception until the end of first year of life). The daily locations are mapped to historically assessed pollution data to make daily exposure values. These are then aggregated based on life stage.

Along with aggregating daily exposure values, ALGAE attempts to assess daily exposure error values and one of five mutually exclusive data quality measure of each value. The exposure error is based on an opportunity cost mechanism that is applied to any day that is involved in a gap or overlap in address periods that may occur in the original residential address histories.

The data quality measures are expressed in numbers of days. Scientists would use the day counts for various categories (e.g.: days spent at an invalid address, at an out of bounds address etc.) and the total days for a given life stage to produce thresholds of data quality that would let them filter results as is appropriate for their studies.

ALGAE's reliance on daily exposure values allows it to support multiple assessment methods. These methods will vary based on whether they include days that have been affected by data cleaning, and whether they use contributions from all locations or just those that happen at key dates.
